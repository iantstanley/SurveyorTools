Stanley Survey Solutions - Elevation Certificates

Elevation Certificates for Brunswick County, NC

Elevation Certificates are official FEMA documents used to verify compliance with community floodplain management ordinances, to determine proper insurance premium rates, and to support requests for Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA) or Letter of Map Revision based on Fill (LOMR-F). As a land surveyor in Brunswick County, understanding these certificates is essential for helping property owners manage flood risks and comply with regulations.

FEMA Building Diagram Reference

The following building diagrams correspond to the most common building types specified on FEMA Elevation Certificates. Understanding which diagram applies to a structure is crucial for accurate certification.

Diagram 1: Slab on Grade

Building on slab-on-grade with no basement. First floor at ground level.

Identification Features:

  • No visible elevation above surrounding grade
  • No crawlspace or basement
  • Finished floor is at or very near ground level
  • Foundation is slab on compacted fill

Documentation Requirements:

When documenting this building type, measure the top of the bottom floor (C2.a) which is typically at the top of the slab. Also document the highest adjacent grade (C2.f) and lowest adjacent grade (C2.e).

Common in Brunswick County:

This building type is common in non-coastal areas of Brunswick County, particularly in newer subdivisions built outside of special flood hazard areas.

Diagram 2: Raised Slab

Building with a raised slab foundation above grade or slab-on-stem-wall with fill.

Identification Features:

  • Slab is visibly raised above surrounding grade
  • Often has steps leading to entrance
  • No crawlspace or accessible area under the slab
  • Typically built on compacted fill with stem walls

Documentation Requirements:

Measure the top of the bottom floor (C2.a) at the top of the raised slab. Document the top of the stem wall or foundation. Carefully document highest adjacent grade (C2.f) and lowest adjacent grade (C2.e).

Common in Brunswick County:

This building type is frequently found in moderate flood risk areas of Brunswick County where some elevation is desired but full elevation on piers isn't required.

Diagram 3: With Basement

Building with a full or partial basement below grade on at least one side.

Identification Features:

  • Has below-grade space on at least one side
  • Often has basement windows or walkout access
  • Finished or unfinished lower level
  • May have a slab-on-grade portion as well

Documentation Requirements:

Document both the top of the basement floor (C2.b) and the top of the next higher floor (C2.a). The bottom of the basement floor joist (C2.c) must also be measured. Pay careful attention to the highest adjacent grade (C2.f) relative to basement openings.

Common in Brunswick County:

This building type is uncommon in coastal areas of Brunswick County due to high water tables, but may be found in inland areas with suitable soil conditions.

Diagram 5: Elevated with Crawlspace

Building with an elevated floor over a crawlspace, where the crawlspace is enclosed by solid perimeter walls.

Identification Features:

  • Elevated first floor with crawlspace below
  • Perimeter walls extend from ground to bottom of first floor
  • Often has vents or small access doors in perimeter walls
  • Typically 2-4 feet of clearance in the crawlspace

Documentation Requirements:

Measure top of the first floor (C2.a), the floor of the crawlspace (C2.d), and verify whether proper flood vents are present. Document the square footage of the crawlspace and the area of flood openings (Section G).

Common in Brunswick County:

Very common in Brunswick County, especially in low to moderate flood risk areas. The crawlspace design provides protection from minor flooding while allowing utilities to be installed below the living space.

Diagram 6: Elevated with Enclosure

Building with elevated living area and a fully enclosed area below that is not a crawlspace.

Identification Features:

  • Elevated living space with a full-height enclosure below
  • Enclosure may be used for parking, storage, or building access
  • Enclosure walls may have flood openings/vents
  • Distinguishable from Diagram 7 by the use of solid perimeter walls rather than posts/piles/piers

Documentation Requirements:

Measure the top of the bottom floor (C2.a) at the elevated living floor. Document the enclosure floor (C2.d) and verify presence of proper flood openings (Section G). Document the use of the enclosure space in Section A8.

Common in Brunswick County:

Common in A-zone areas of Brunswick County. Often built to provide garage or storage space below with the living area safely elevated above the Base Flood Elevation.

Diagram 7: Elevated on Piles/Piers

Building elevated on posts, piles, piers, or columns with open area below the elevated floor.

Identification Features:

  • Elevated structure on piles, piers, or columns
  • Open area beneath structure (may have partial enclosure)
  • Often has steps or stairs to access elevated living area
  • Typically elevated 8+ feet in coastal areas

Documentation Requirements:

Measure top of bottom floor (C2.a) at the elevated living floor. If there are enclosures, document their square footage and flood vent details (Section G). For coastal areas, document the bottom of the lowest horizontal structural member (C2.c).

Common in Brunswick County:

Very common in coastal VE zones of Brunswick County, including beach communities like Oak Island, Ocean Isle Beach, and Holden Beach. Required in high-risk coastal areas to allow storm surge to pass beneath the structure.

Diagram 8: Elevated with Subgrade Crawlspace

Elevated building with a crawlspace that is partially or fully below grade.

Identification Features:

  • Elevated first floor with crawlspace below
  • Crawlspace is partially or fully below grade on all sides
  • Often has vents or small access doors
  • The grade slopes away from the foundation

Documentation Requirements:

Measure the top of the first floor (C2.a), the crawlspace floor (C2.d), and verify whether proper flood vents are present. Document both the highest adjacent grade (C2.f) and lowest adjacent grade (C2.e) in relation to the crawlspace.

Common in Brunswick County:

Found in some inland areas of Brunswick County with sloping lots where the crawlspace may be partially below grade on the uphill side.

Diagram 9: Non-elevated with Subgrade Crawlspace

Building with a crawlspace below grade but without significant elevation of the first floor.

Identification Features:

  • First floor close to grade level
  • Crawlspace is below grade on all sides
  • Often has vents or small access doors
  • Similar to Diagram 8 but without significant elevation

Documentation Requirements:

Measure the top of the first floor (C2.a), the crawlspace floor (C2.d), and document the highest adjacent grade (C2.f) and lowest adjacent grade (C2.e). Verify whether proper flood vents are present.

Common in Brunswick County:

Less common in flood-prone areas of Brunswick County, but may be found in older homes in slightly elevated inland areas built before current flood regulations.

Note: These diagrams correspond to the building types defined on the FEMA Elevation Certificate. When completing an Elevation Certificate, select the diagram that most closely resembles the subject building. Additional measurements and certifications may be required based on the specific details of the structure.
